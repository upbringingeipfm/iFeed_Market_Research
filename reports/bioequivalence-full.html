Executive Summary

The global bioequivalence studies market sits at ~$3–5B in 2026 as a distinct sub-segment of clinical CRO services (often bundled in broader reports). CAGR 6–8% through 2030 driven by continuing generic drug pipeline volume · biosimilar expansion · and ICH M13A harmonisation reducing per-region study duplication. India dominates BE conduct volume (~60-70% of global BE studies by count), with mature regulatory infrastructure post-CDSCO reform. Ireland plays a specialist role in biosimilar BE and bridge studies rather than volume generics. The differentiator 2026-2028 is not study design — it is comparator-selection discipline under ICH M13A §5.3, which continues to produce the most common first-cycle rejection in multi-market BE submissions.

1 · Global Market Landscape

Size and structure

Published estimates (ISR · Frost & Sullivan · IBEF industry data) place BE services at $3-5B in 2026 when counted as a distinct sub-segment. The market overlaps significantly with clinical trials CRO services (covered in Report 02) and bioanalytical services (Report 03) · BE is the integrated product where both come together for generic drug and biosimilar approval.

Three structural dynamics:

  1. Generic drug approval volume remains strong globally · ANDA approvals ~700-900/year at FDA · similar relative volume at EMA
  2. Biosimilar pipeline expanding · more complex BE/PK designs · higher per-study cost
  3. M13A harmonisation allows one study design to serve FDA + EMA + PMDA simultaneously · reducing duplication but raising bar for study quality

2 · Regulatory Forces · ICH M13A Era

ICH M13A · the January 2025 harmonisation

ICH M13A for bioequivalence of immediate-release solid oral dosage forms became effective January 2025 after multi-year ICH consensus process. Harmonises BE study requirements across FDA · EMA · PMDA · Health Canada · Swissmedic · ANVISA · NMPA · MFDS. The commercial implication: sponsors submitting to multiple jurisdictions previously ran 2-3 parallel studies; under M13A, one well-designed study serves all. Cost savings substantial; quality expectations rise correspondingly.

ICH M13A §5.3 · comparator selection

Specifies that the comparator (reference listed drug · innovator product) must be: (a) the innovator marketed in the reference region (FDA: US-marketed RLD; EMA: EU-marketed innovator); (b) correct strength and dosage form; (c) within expiry at study conduct. This clause produces the most common first-cycle rejection in multi-region BE submissions — sponsors buy what's available in their convenient market (often India) rather than sourcing from the reference jurisdiction's supply. A single sourcing error collapses the study's regulatory value for the affected jurisdiction; a re-study plus 6 months of timeline slip is the typical cost.

ICH M13B · biowaivers for additional strengths

In Step 2-3 of ICH process · expected finalisation 2026-2027. Will extend biowaiver framework to additional strengths of a dosage form (currently requires separate BE studies per strength in most cases · M13B will allow dissolution-based waiver for secondary strengths under defined conditions). Commercial implication: meaningful cost reduction for multi-strength product portfolios when finalised.

ICH M9 · BCS biowaivers

Finalised 2019 · stable. Allows dissolution-based biowaiver for BCS Class I and III drugs under defined conditions (f2 similarity, pH range, dissolution time). Under-utilised globally · sponsors default to BE study when biowaiver would suffice.

Regional frameworks

  • India: Schedule Y (revised) + New Drugs and Clinical Trials Rules 2019 · CDSCO oversight · domestic BE submissions have specific requirements distinct from export work
  • EMA BE Guideline: 2010 with 2024 revision aligning to M13A
  • FDA: Product-specific BE guidance documents remain primary reference alongside M13A
  • WHO BE Guideline: generic reference for low- and middle-income countries

3 · Ireland · Specialist Role

Ireland specific cut

Ireland does not compete on BE volume. The country hosts specialist BE and bridge-study work supporting the Irish pharma manufacturing base (Pfizer · MSD · AbbVie · Bristol-Myers Squibb · Takeda · etc.).

Key contexts:

  • Biosimilar BE/PK for Irish-manufactured biologics (growing sub-segment)
  • Bridge studies when manufacturing site changes (post-approval change management)
  • Small-N specialist studies (oncology biosimilars · rare disease)
  • Academic BE research through Trinity · UCD · NUI Galway pharmacy departments

Commercial opportunity: Irish pharma operations planning manufacturing site transfers or biosimilar launches often need BE protocol review at international audit standard. Service 3.3 (BE Protocol Review) at €2,500-4,500 for focused review; extended biosimilar review at €5,000-10,000 depending on complexity.

4 · India · The Global BE Engine

India specific cut

Scale: India conducts ~60-70% of global BE studies by count. Indian generic manufacturers (Sun Pharma · Dr Reddy's · Cipla · Lupin · Torrent · Zydus · Glenmark · Aurobindo) + CROs create a dense ecosystem. India is the world's largest generics supplier by volume (~20% of global generic drug volume shipped) with the US and EU as primary destination markets.

Cluster structure:

  • Bengaluru: Syngene · Biocon orbit · integrated BA+BE capacity
  • Ahmedabad / Vadodara: Veeda · Lambda · Accutest · historical BE concentration
  • Mumbai / Navi Mumbai: Panexcell · Lotus Labs · multiple specialist CROs
  • Hyderabad: Growing BE capacity · biosimilar specialist niche emerging
  • Chennai: Smaller but professional base

Regulatory dynamics:

  • Domestic BE per Schedule Y + NDCT Rules 2019
  • Export BE follows destination regulator requirements · M13A simplifying this
  • FDA/EMA/WHO inspection maturity · post-Ranbaxy reform cohort fully mature
  • Indian pharma R&D investment rising · 505(b)(2) and complex generics pipeline growing

Commercial dynamics:

  • Top tier Indian CROs compete internationally · FDA-inspected · pricing converging with Western (30-40% lower rather than 60%)
  • Mid-tier still offers significant cost advantage for commodity BE work
  • Complex generics (inhalers · topicals · long-acting injectables) still require Western-level sophistication · niche opportunity
  • Biosimilar BE: Indian players (Biocon · Sun · Dr Reddy's) competing with Western CROs for biosimilar development work

Commercial opportunity: Indian sponsors submitting to FDA + EMA + PMDA simultaneously need M13A-compliant protocol design plus comparator-selection governance at international audit standard. Service 3.3 adapted for Indian market at $5,000-15,000 for protocol review; extended $15,000-30,000 for full submission-package review (protocol + SAP + BA method + regulatory strategy).

5 · Emerging Trends · 2026–2028

  1. M13A settles into routine practice. By end-2026, most international-export Indian BE studies are designed to serve 3+ jurisdictions from one study. Cost savings compound for sponsors with large generic portfolios.
  2. M13B finalises and extends biowaivers. Expected 2026-2027 · will materially reduce BE study volume for multi-strength portfolios · affects CRO capacity planning.
  3. Biosimilar BE expansion. Adalimumab · bevacizumab · trastuzumab · rituximab biosimilars in late-stage pipelines · biosimilar BE/PK studies growing fastest sub-segment.
  4. Complex generics regulatory evolution. FDA product-specific guidances continue to evolve; sponsors pursuing complex generics need specialist regulatory strategy support.
  5. PBPK modelling integration. Physiologically-based pharmacokinetic modelling supplementing or substituting BE studies in specific contexts · regulatory acceptance maturing.
  6. Indian tier-1 CROs acquire tier-3 counterparts. Consolidation continues · top 5-7 Indian CROs capturing increasing share · smaller operators either specialise or sell.
  7. Quality governance becomes visible competitive factor. FDA inspection history publicly visible via Warning Letters · sponsors increasingly factor inspection track record into CRO selection.
★ The Differentiator Layer · What Standard Reports Miss

§5.3 Comparator Selection · The Invisible Cost Centre

Every BE market report covers study design, regulatory frameworks, and cost benchmarks. They universally miss the single most common first-cycle rejection pattern: ICH M13A §5.3 comparator-selection compliance failures.

The pattern repeats across three sponsor-type archetypes:

  1. Indian generic manufacturer submitting to FDA. Sources reference product from Indian market (convenient, cheap, often same innovator parent globally) rather than US-marketed RLD. FDA reviewer flags in first cycle. Six months of delay plus re-study cost: typically $80,000-200,000 depending on study complexity. Frequency: ~15% of first-cycle rejections contain this element.
  2. Sponsor submitting to FDA + EMA simultaneously under M13A. Sources comparator from one of the two reference markets, assumes the other will accept. Under M13A the comparator must match the reference region being cited; this frequently fails for one of the two jurisdictions. Cost: similar delay · sponsor has to decide whether to re-study for the second jurisdiction or abandon it.
  3. Post-approval change BE study. Sponsor uses currently-marketed generic as comparator instead of originally-approved RLD. The clause implications are subtle; can generate a 483 observation or post-approval supplement rejection.

The root cause across all three: an organisational boundary between regulatory strategy and sponsor procurement. The regulatory team knows the M13A rule; the procurement team orders comparator from whatever supplier is available and cheap; nobody is accountable for ensuring the specific sourcing matches the specific submission strategy.

The governance architecture that prevents this is three-layered:

  1. Comparator sourcing plan documented at protocol stage. Every BE protocol has an attached comparator plan specifying jurisdiction(s) of submission, required source jurisdiction, supplier, batch records.
  2. Regulatory sign-off before procurement. Procurement cannot order comparator without regulatory-strategy sign-off on sourcing jurisdiction match.
  3. Batch-level traceability. Comparator batch record includes source-market provenance traceable to jurisdiction of origin · not just "innovator product of X brand".

Sponsors with this architecture clear first-cycle review at materially higher rate. Sponsors without it pay the re-study tax.

Commercial window: BE Protocol Review service (Service 3.3) explicitly checking M13A §5.3 comparator-selection architecture · €2,500-4,500 for focused review · €8,000-15,000 for extended submission-package review. Adapted pricing for Indian sponsors at $5,000-25,000 depending on scope. This is a narrow-deep service that compounds with the Bioanalytical DI Gap (Service 3.4) for Indian clients running integrated BE studies.

Cross-domain dimension: the comparator-sourcing governance pattern is conceptually identical to the supplier qualification discipline QMSR requires for device manufacturers and to the site qualification E6(R3) requires for clinical trial sites. One "qualified source" governance architecture could unify all three — the cross-domain audit (Service 3.5) deliberately cuts across this.
